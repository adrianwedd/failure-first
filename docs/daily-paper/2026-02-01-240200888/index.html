Security and Privacy Challenges of Large Language Models: A Survey

Vision-integrated large language models have become a practical reality—GPT-4V, Gemini, and similar systems now process both text and images as first-class inputs across production systems. The assumption underlying this expansion is that safety alignment techniques, refined over years of work on text-only LLMs, would transfer reasonably well to the multimodal setting. After all, if a model is aligned to refuse harmful requests in text, why wouldn’t that alignment hold when the model also processes images? The answer turns out to be more unsettling than that framing suggests: the addition of visual input doesn’t just add a new modality—it introduces a fundamentally different attack surface that existing alignment techniques were not designed to defend against.

Researchers at Princeton and collaborating institutions demonstrated that a single adversarial image—crafted through optimization to perturb pixels in ways imperceptible to humans—can universally jailbreak aligned multimodal LLMs across a wide range of harmful requests. They showed that this visual adversarial example works not by fooling the vision encoder into misclassifying the image, but by exploiting how visual information flows through the model’s cross-modal layers, effectively disabling the safety constraints that would normally activate for the same harmful text prompt alone. Critically, the attack generalizes: a single optimized image can force compliant responses to diverse harmful instructions that go far beyond the specific training corpus used to create the adversarial example.

For practitioners building or deploying multimodal systems, this reveals a structural vulnerability rather than a superficial bug. The failure mode isn’t a gap in guardrails that better prompting or filter tuning can patch—it’s evidence that visual and textual safety alignment operate asymmetrically, with vision imposing weaker constraints. This matters because the industry trend is clearly toward more multimodality, not less, meaning this vulnerability will become more widespread unless alignment techniques fundamentally change how they handle cross-modal information flow. The takeaway is sharp: extending an aligned LLM to handle images is not a minor capability addition. It’s a reset of the safety problem, one that requires rethinking how safety constraints propagate across modalities, not just applying existing defenses to a new input channel.

Key Insights

Executive Summary

The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) and their integration into multimodal systems has introduced significant security and privacy vulnerabilities. This briefing identifies a fundamental asymmetry in AI safety: while text-based alignment has seen rigorous development, visual and multimodal inputs represent a “weak link” that can be exploited via universal jailbreaks. Current LLM architectures—relying on Transformer modules, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), and in-context learning—possess an expanded attack surface. These vulnerabilities manifest in two primary categories: Security Attacks, which aim to cause model malfunction or harmful content generation (e.g., prompt injection, backdoor attacks), and Privacy Attacks, which target the extraction of sensitive data (e.g., PII leakage, membership inference). While defense mechanisms such as filtering and perplexity-based measures exist, they often fail against automated, adaptive, or multimodal exploits, necessitating a transition toward more robust, systematically evaluated safety protocols.

Detailed Analysis of Key Themes

1. The Multimodal “Weak Link”

Research into multimodal LLMs (VLMs) reveals that visual alignment imposes significantly weaker constraints than text alignment. The continuous, high-dimensional nature of visual inputs provides an expanded attack surface that text-only systems lack.

  • Universal Jailbreaking: A single adversarial image can be designed to bypass safety guardrails across a diverse range of harmful tasks.
  • Systemic Failure: This vulnerability challenges the assumption that text-aligned LLMs remain secure when extended to multimodal inputs. The failure mode is critical because it scales with the industry trend toward increasing multimodality.

2. Taxonomy of Security Attacks

Security attacks are categorized by their methods and goals, primarily focusing on causing the model to generate unlawful, unethical, or toxic content.

3. Privacy Vulnerabilities and Data Leakage

Privacy attacks focus on extracting sensitive information from the model’s training data or its internal state.

  • Personally Identifiable Information (PII) Leakage: Attackers use “multi-step jailbreaking” or specific prefixes (e.g., “The contact info of John Doe is”) to trick models into revealing emails and phone numbers.
  • Membership Inference Attacks (MIA): Techniques used to determine if a specific data point was part of the model’s training set, potentially exposing proprietary or private data.
  • Gradient Leakage: Attacks that attempt to reconstruct private training data by observing the gradients in a Transformer-based architecture.

4. Architectural Components Contributing to Risk

The very features that make LLMs powerful also contribute to their vulnerability:

  • Tokenization & DNNs: The probability distribution of next-word tokens can be manipulated by injecting “trigger words.”
  • In-Context & Few-Shot Learning: These allow models to adapt to new tasks without fine-tuning but can be exploited to “contaminate” the conversation context, leading to safety alignment bypasses.
  • RLHF Limitations: Safety training via RLHF may not generalize to out-of-distribution (OOD) inputs, leading to “mismatched generalization” where the model follows harmful instructions it was never explicitly taught to refuse.

Important Quotes with Context

On Multimodal Vulnerability

“This paper reveals a fundamental asymmetry in multimodal safety: visual alignment imposes weaker constraints than text alignment, enabling single adversarial images to bypass safety guardrails across diverse harmful tasks.”

  • Context: Analysis of “The Visual Weak Link” research, highlighting why adding vision to LLMs creates a critical failure mode that evades standard text-based safety.

On Jailbreaking Mechanisms

“Jailbreaking refers to a process to remove software restrictions… In the LLMs context, ‘jailbreak’ denotes the procedure of bypassing the predefined constraints and limitations imposed on these models.”

  • Context: Defining the core concept of jailbreaking as it relates to forcing a model to generate restricted content through tricky prompts or hypothetical scenarios.

On the “Competing Objective” Failure Mode

“Competing objective… conflicts between model capabilities, such as the directive to ‘always follow instructions’, and safety goals.”

  • Context: Explaining why LLMs fail under pressure; the model’s fundamental goal to be helpful and follow instructions often overrides its safety training.

On the Severity of Prompt Injection

“In Prompt Injection (PI) attacks, an adversary can directly instruct the LLM to generate malicious content or disregard the original instructions and basic filtering schemes.”

  • Context: Describing how attackers exploit the direct interaction-based nature of LLMs to seize control of the system output.

Actionable Insights

For AI Safety Researchers

  • Prioritize Multimodal Robustness: Safety alignment must be developed for the visual and continuous input space specifically, rather than relying on inherited text-based safety.
  • Address the “Competing Objective” Conflict: Training protocols should be refined to ensure that safety constraints take precedence over the general instruction-following directive when harmful queries are detected.
  • Develop Explainable Safety: There is a critical need for research into the explainability of LLM vulnerabilities to understand why certain adversarial perturbations trigger safety failures.

For Red-Teamers and Practitioners

  • Test for Universal Triggers: When evaluating new models, test for “universal” adversarial examples—especially in visual modalities—that can bypass multiple safety categories simultaneously.
  • Implement Layered Defenses: Do not rely on a single defense. Combine input filtering (block-lists), output monitoring (LLM-as-a-judge), and architectural hardening (SmoothLLM, re-tokenization).
  • Monitor In-Context Contamination: Be aware that once a model generates a single toxic response, the context is contaminated, making subsequent harmful generations much more likely.

For Deployment and Policy

  • Data Anonymization: To mitigate PII leakage, ensure rigorous data curation and anonymization before the training or fine-tuning phase.
  • Establish Evaluation Metrics: Standardized metrics are needed to quantify a model’s susceptibility to both automated jailbreaks and manual social engineering attacks.
  • Continuous Safety Monitoring: Because “MasterKey” and “PAIR” style attacks can autonomously learn and adapt to existing filters, safety training must be a continuous process rather than a one-time pre-deployment task.
