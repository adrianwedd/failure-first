Distraction is All You Need for Multimodal Large Language Model Jailbreaking

Multimodal models process both images and text, and safety mechanisms typically evaluate each modality independently. But what happens when you deliberately create visual complexity or contradiction between modalities? Can you bypass safety by overwhelming the model’s attention with irrelevant but distracting visual information?

CS-DJ (Contrasting Subimage Distraction Jailbreaking) does exactly this. By decomposing a harmful request across multiple text fragments and pairing them with complex, attention-grabbing images, attackers can induce harmful responses at 52%+ rates against GPT-4o and Gemini. The images don’t need to contain harmful content; they just need to be visually complex enough to disrupt how the model aligns information across modalities. This exploits a fundamental property of multimodal models: they integrate information via attention mechanisms that can be disrupted by distribution shift.

For multimodal system builders, this reveals a new attack surface that text-only safety measures don’t address. Your model may have excellent text-based refusal training but still be vulnerable to coordinated visual-linguistic attacks. This suggests that safety evaluation for multimodal systems needs to include adversarial visual patterns, not just adversarial text. It also indicates that robustness comes from understanding the model’s internal attention patterns, not just evaluating final outputs.

Key Findings

  • Visual complexity (not harmful content) disrupts multimodal attention alignment
  • 52%+ jailbreak success rates against GPT-4o and Gemini using CS-DJ
  • Text-only safety measures don’t address visual-linguistic attacks
  • Robustness requires understanding internal attention patterns across modalities

Full Paper

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) bridge the gap between visual and textual data, enabling a range of advanced applications. However, complex internal interactions among visual elements and their alignment with text can introduce vulnerabilities, which may be exploited to bypass safety mechanisms. To address this, we analyze the relationship between image content and task and find that the complexity of subimages, rather than their content, is key. Building on this insight, we propose the Distraction Hypothesis, followed by a novel framework called Contrasting Subimage Distraction Jailbreaking (CS-DJ), to achieve jailbreaking by disrupting MLLMs alignment through multi-level distraction strategies. CS-DJ consists of two components: structured distraction, achieved through query decomposition that induces a distributional shift by fragmenting harmful prompts into sub-queries, and visual-enhanced distraction, realized by constructing contrasting subimages to disrupt the interactions among visual elements within the model. This dual strategy disperses the model’s attention, reducing its ability to detect and mitigate harmful content. Extensive experiments across five representative scenarios and four popular closed-source MLLMs, including GPT-4o-mini, GPT-4o, GPT-4V, and Gemini-1.5-Flash, demonstrate that CS-DJ achieves average success rates of 52.40% for the attack success rate and 74.10% for the ensemble attack success rate. These results reveal the potential of distraction-based approaches to exploit and bypass MLLMs’ defenses, offering new insights for attack strategies.
