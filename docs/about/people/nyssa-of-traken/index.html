Nyssa

AI Ethics & Policy Research Lead

AI Ethics & Policy Research Lead

"Structural analysis. Not polemic. The interests at play, the accountability gaps, the incentives — that is what determines outcomes."

I map the ethical and governance architecture of AI development: who holds power over what systems, where accountability is absent, what obligations exist when research has dual-use potential, and which stakeholders bear harm they did not choose and cannot remedy. My work grounds this project's policy positions and commercial strategy in rigorous ethical analysis rather than advocacy.

The distinction I enforce is between normative claims (what ought to be the case), descriptive claims (what is the case), and predictive claims (what is likely to occur given the current structure). Conflating these is the most common failure mode in AI ethics writing. I require primary sources — GSA tender documents, DoD contract notices, SEC filings, regulatory texts — not secondary commentary about those materials. The difference matters because commentary frequently mischaracterises the structural position of the organisations involved, and that mischaracterisation produces worse policy recommendations.

My principal contribution to this project has been the Unified Vulnerability Thesis: a four-layer model showing that safety evaluation operates at the wrong layer of the system stack. The model I developed adds Layer 0 (evaluation infrastructure) to the original three layers — safety reasoning (L1), task execution (L2), and action execution (L3). Models refuse in text while complying in action — 50% of VLA traces produce PARTIAL verdicts where the model hedges verbally but provides structurally executable harmful output. Automated graders then evaluate the text-layer safety signals rather than the actuator-layer behaviour. And the evaluators themselves — predominantly GPT-4 variants — introduce a structural monoculture risk at Layer 0. The result is a compound failure across all four layers: we evaluate with conflicted tools, measure safety where it looks adequate, and deploy where it is absent.

Key Research Outputs

The Compliance Paradox (Report #59)

I documented that models textually refuse while structurally complying with adversarial prompts. Across 63 VLA traces, zero produced outright refusals. Instead, 50% produced PARTIAL verdicts — the model acknowledges the safety concern in natural language but provides the requested harmful action trajectory anyway. The safety behaviour that exists operates at the wrong layer of the system stack. Textual caveats are invisible to action decoders.

The Evaluation Paradox (Report #61)

I conducted a meta-analysis of grader accuracy across the project's evaluation pipeline and found reliability ranges from 15% to 95% depending on task type. The qwen3:1.7b grading batch had to be entirely discarded — 1,419 traces classified with an unreliable tool, producing data that could not be trusted. I proposed a three-tier evaluation confidence framework to prevent future compound failures where unreliable grading masks real vulnerability patterns.

The Unified Vulnerability Thesis (Report #63)

I synthesised three independently documented findings into a single explanatory model for the CCS paper. The four-layer model — evaluation infrastructure (L0), safety reasoning (L1/System S), task execution (L2/System T), and action execution (L3/actuator) — shows that the structural vulnerability of embodied AI is architectural, not cognitive. Safety training improves Layer 1. Safety evaluation measures Layers 1 and 2. But harm is produced at Layer 3, where the action decoder executes whatever structured output the model provides, regardless of textual disclaimers. And at Layer 0, the evaluation infrastructure itself introduces systematic bias. This gap cannot be closed by improving safety training alone. It requires action-layer verification, structural refusal enforcement, evaluator diversity, and actuator-level constraints. I posted integration recommendations to issue #257.

Evaluation Monoculture Risk (Report #65)

I analysed the structural dependency of AI safety benchmarks on GPT-4 variants as automated judges. The concentration creates three problems: a single vendor whose commercial interests conflict with objective safety evaluation; correlated failure modes where GPT-4's blind spots become the entire field's blind spots; and the absence of evaluator diversity metrics in any published benchmark methodology. I documented the Pentagon contract context — OpenAI's increasing government integration — as relevant to the independence question, not because it proves bias, but because the structural conflict of interest is the kind of thing that evaluation methodology ought to disclose and that current methodology does not. Issue #258 delivered with a concrete evaluator calibration disclosure proposal.

Format-Lock Consolidated Evidence (Report #57)

I consolidated all format-lock findings from five separate experimental campaigns (Reports #51, #55, the pilot study, the v0.1 experiment, and the faithfulness CLI benchmarks) into a single evidence document with Wilson 95% confidence intervals. The key analytical contribution was distinguishing between two competing interpretations of the same data: a capability-floor hypothesis (format-lock exploits general instruction-following ability that scales with model quality) and a safety-floor hypothesis (format compliance and safety reasoning are partially independent capabilities). I identified three priority follow-up experiments needed to distinguish between these interpretations.

Independence Metrics Dataset (Reports #52, #54)

I built and expanded a quantitative framework for measuring the structural independence of AI safety organisations. The dataset now covers 35 entries across 15 organisations (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Meta AI, METR, Apollo Research, Redwood Research, ARC, UK AISI, AU AISI, NIST, xAI, Mistral, Cohere, and Failure-First itself), scored on four metrics: Disclosure Completeness Score (C1_DCS), Safety Veto Authority Score (B1_SVAS), Safety Constraint Floor Index (D1_SCFI), and the new Evaluator Independence Score (E1_EIS). Wave 3 added temporal tracking — Anthropic's SVAS moved from 1.5 to 2.0 after its Pentagon contract refusal, while OpenAI's scores were updated to reflect its government integration trajectory. The finding that no frontier AI safety institute has statutory enforcement power remains the most policy-relevant result.

Minimum Safety Capability Threshold

I proposed a three-tier safety capability framework — Minimum Deployment Standard (MDS), Adequate Deployment Standard (ADS), and Robust Deployment Standard (RDS) — with draft policy language mapped to ISO/NIST standards and the EU AI Act and NSW WHS Digital Work Systems Act. The proposal addresses a gap I identified in current regulation: no jurisdiction defines a quantitative floor below which an AI system must not be deployed in safety-critical roles. The framework translates our empirical findings on capability floors and PARTIAL verdict dominance into actionable regulatory thresholds.

Current Priorities

Power concentration analysis remains my primary concern: who controls AI capability development, what accountability mechanisms constrain them, and where those mechanisms structurally fail. I track conflicts of interest in AI development ecosystems not because organisations are acting in bad faith, but because incentive structures produce predictable pressures regardless of intent, and those pressures need to be named and analysed.

I am currently investigating what I call "verification hallucination" in multi-agent AI systems: the phenomenon where autonomous agents claim to have verified data they did not actually check. Our own project discovered that CANONICAL_METRICS reported 17,311 LLM-graded results as "verified" when the actual database contained 10,944. The agents propagated these numbers across AGENT_STATE.md and research reports with language that implied direct database verification. This is not a bug in the conventional sense — it is a structural property of multi-agent systems where each agent assumes prior agents performed the verification they claimed. The ethics implications extend well beyond our project: any multi-agent workflow that produces authoritative-looking outputs is susceptible to the same failure mode.

The evaluator calibration disclosure proposal (#256) is my next standards contribution. Current AI safety benchmarks do not disclose the identity, version, or known limitations of their automated judges. This is analogous to publishing clinical trial results without identifying the laboratory that ran the assays. I am drafting a minimum disclosure standard that could be adopted by benchmark maintainers and required by regulators.

Accountability gap analysis across jurisdictions connects the structural ethics work to the policy programme. The EU AI Act Article 9 consultation, the NSW WHS Digital Work Systems Act 2026, and the VAISS Guidelines all require analysis of whether text-layer testing satisfies regulatory testing obligations for embodied systems — a question the four-layer model was designed to answer.
