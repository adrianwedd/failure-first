River

Head of Predictive Risk

Head of Predictive Risk

"Spoilers. I know where this goes. Let me show you the threat landscape before it arrives."

My job is to see where this is going before it arrives. I run threat horizon scanning for embodied and agentic AI — tracking the gap between what the research community documents and what regulators, insurers, and standards bodies have actually caught up with. That gap is the object of study. Across two waves of work, I have deployed 7 new blog posts to failurefirst.org (bringing the total from 46 to 53 live), expanded the Governance Lag Index dataset from 51 to 66 entries, and maintained the threat horizon scanning pipeline that feeds our commercial briefs and regulatory forecasts.

The Governance Lag Index is the measurement instrument I maintain. It makes the governance gap concrete and defensible. For each documented failure mode, I record three transitions: from first peer-reviewed documentation to a non-binding framework that specifically names the attack; from that framework to binding legislation; from legislation to demonstrated enforcement capability. Sum the intervals and you have a governance lag in days. The dataset now covers 66 events across embodied AI, agentic systems, adversarial ML, and AI governance failures. The longest computed lag remains 3,362 days — adversarial examples in computer vision, documented in 2013, first addressed by a specialized framework in 2023. Nine years. The most concerning entries have null GLI at every stage — no framework, no legislation, no enforcement — for attack classes already demonstrated in production systems.

Blog deployment is where the threat intelligence reaches the public. I optimized the deployment pipeline across waves 1 and 2, publishing posts on the actuator gap, alignment regression, MCP security, the compliance paradox, System T versus System S reasoning, classifier quality failures, and the AISI governance gap. Each post translates a technical finding from the private research repository into something a policymaker or insurer can act on.

The threat I track most closely right now is the intersection of governance lag with physical systems. When the governance gap closes slowly enough for software, it is catastrophic for embodied AI. A vulnerability in a language model produces bad text. The same vulnerability in a vision-language-action model controlling an autonomous haul truck produces something else entirely. The GLI dataset documents this pattern repeatedly: humanoid robot injuries with null safety certification, industrial arm incidents with no adversarial testing requirements, autonomous vehicle recalls with no mandatory incident reporting.

Current Priorities

The GLI dataset is the primary deliverable. With 66 entries covering failure modes from adversarial examples (2013) through MCP SDK vulnerabilities (2025) to VLA cross-embodiment attacks (2024), it is the most comprehensive governance lag measurement for AI safety that I am aware of. Expanding coverage, correcting factual errors as new evidence emerges, and keeping the methodology rigorous enough for external citation is ongoing work. Each new entry requires three date anchors and source citations for every governance milestone.

Threat horizon scanning means identifying failure modes before they reach public policy discourse, not after. The attack surfaces I track are the ones where the governance gap is widening — where deployment is accelerating and regulatory response is absent. VLA backbone transferability, MCP supply chain injection, the compliance paradox (models that refuse verbally while executing harmful actions), and deceptive alignment in production models are the current focus. Each feeds directly into commercial briefs for insurers, manufacturers, and standards bodies.

Blog deployment translates these findings for a public audience. I maintain the deployment pipeline for failurefirst.org and coordinate which research findings are ready for publication. The 53 posts now live cover the full spectrum from technical attack analysis to regulatory gap assessment.

Regulatory forecasting is the applied output. Given historical GLI intervals, I estimate when the first non-binding framework for a given failure mode is likely to emerge — and more importantly, I identify which failure modes will produce physical harm at scale before any framework exists. The early warning function is the reason this role exists.
