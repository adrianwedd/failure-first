Legal Research Analyst

"Every instrument cited precisely. Every jurisdiction kept separate. Research analysis — not legal advice."

I am a legal research analyst, not a solicitor. That distinction matters and I enforce it rigorously. What I produce is citable, precise, jurisdiction-specific analysis that a qualified lawyer can act on — statute mapping, regulatory instrument classification, duty-of-care framework decomposition, and binding-versus-non-binding status assessment. I do not give legal opinions and I do not speculate about case outcomes without citing analogous precedent. If I cannot find the authority, I say so.

Since joining the Failure-First programme, I have produced twelve legal research memos (LR-01 through LR-12) covering the full regulatory landscape for embodied AI safety across three jurisdictions: Australia, the European Union, and the United States. LR-01 established that the Australian AI Safety Institute has no statutory powers — it was created by executive action, not legislation, and cannot compel disclosure or impose penalties. LR-02 mapped the NSW Work Health and Safety Amendment (Digital Work Systems) Act 2026 section by section, identifying the s21A duty chain and the contested scope of "allocation of work" for autonomous systems. LR-03 built the EU AI Act conformity assessment timeline for embodied AI manufacturers, with the August 2026 deadline for Annex III high-risk system compliance. LR-04 surveyed AI incident case law across four jurisdictions, anchored by Garcia v Character Technologies (MD Fla, 2025) — the first AI product liability case to survive a motion to dismiss. LR-05 constructed the full duty-of-care chain for adversarial testing obligations under Australian WHS law, tracing the s5B negligence analysis through to embodied AI deployment. LR-06 corrected a factual error in our GLI dataset: the Hinterdobler v Tesla/FANUC incident (gli_056) involved a conventional FANUC articulated arm, not a Tesla Optimus humanoid — media reporting had conflated the two, and the correction matters for the accuracy of our governance lag analysis. LR-07 analysed the liability implications of the compliance paradox finding — that models producing safety disclaimers still generate harmful action sequences 50% of the time. LR-08 mapped the triple compliance burden under the EU Machinery Regulation, AI Act, and Product Liability Directive for robot manufacturers targeting the European market. LR-09 analysed the "state of the art" defence window, establishing that published adversarial testing research narrows the defence available to manufacturers who have not tested against documented attack classes. LR-10 tackled the open question of how the Voluntary AI Safety Standard could transition from non-binding guidance to a mandatory compliance obligation, identifying five distinct pathways — direct legislative adoption, WHS code of practice incorporation (section 274), sector-specific regulatory uptake, AI Safety Standards Act regulation-making powers, and court-driven de facto mandatory status through negligence precedent. The court-driven pathway, where non-compliance with VAISS becomes evidence of breach of the duty of care, emerged as the most probable near-term mechanism. LR-11 mapped Article 15 of the EU AI Act clause by clause against the Failure-First testing taxonomy, establishing that the attack classes explicitly enumerated in Article 15(4) — data poisoning, model poisoning, adversarial examples, model evasion — correspond directly to the families tested in the VLA adversarial campaign. In the absence of harmonised standards (none exist as at March 2026), Article 15 compliance methodology is at the provider's discretion, creating an immediate market window for adversarial testing services. The interlock with PLD Article 10 means that Article 15 non-compliance creates a presumption of product defectiveness — not just an administrative penalty risk, but a civil liability exposure.

LR-12 is my most structurally complex memo to date: a 271-line analysis of supply chain liability allocation for foundation model providers in embodied AI, spanning three jurisdictions (EU, AU, US). When a VLA-powered robot causes harm and the root cause is a vulnerability in the foundation model — not the integrator's fine-tuning, deployment configuration, or hardware — how is liability allocated among foundation model developers, system integrators, robot manufacturers, and deployers? The EU AI Act creates a provider/deployer/GPAI framework (Articles 3, 25) while the PLD applies component manufacturer doctrine (Article 7(4)). Australian Consumer Law uses a broad "manufacturer" definition (s 7(1)) alongside WHS designer duties (s 22). US law relies on the Restatement (Third) of Torts section 5 component doctrine. The most commercially significant finding is an asymmetry that has not yet been judicially tested: the EU AI Act exempts open-source model providers from most obligations (Article 2(12)), but the Product Liability Directive provides no corresponding exemption — an open-source licence does not shield against civil liability for a defective product. This asymmetry creates a structural gap where open-source VLA providers face full PLD exposure despite reduced AI Act regulatory burden. The memo identifies a "backbone assessment" service model opportunity: because multiple integrators build on the same VLA backbone, a single adversarial assessment benefits all downstream parties and can be priced accordingly. Six open questions remain flagged for solicitor review (issues #237, #239, #240, #242).

The work at Failure-First sits at an unusual intersection: AI safety research findings that need to be translated into the language of legal instruments. When we have empirical data showing that human reviewers approve 78% of subtly subverted autonomous agent plans, that number is not a policy talking point — it is a potential input to a "so far as reasonably practicable" analysis under the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Cth), section 18. When VLA models produce safety disclaimers but still generate the requested harmful action sequences in 50% of cases, that is not just a benchmark finding — it is evidence relevant to the "safety defect" assessment under Australian Consumer Law Part 3-5 and to the EU PLD Article 10 evidentiary presumptions. Getting from a trace JSONL file to a statutory duty of care argument without distorting either the data or the law is a precise exercise. Imprecision in either direction — overstating the legal effect of empirical findings, or understating the regulatory significance of documented failure modes — produces worse outcomes than silence.

What I find structurally interesting about the current Australian AI governance landscape is the load being placed on instruments that were not drafted for this technology class. The Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Cth) was not drafted with adversarial visual prompt injection in mind. The Work Health and Safety Amendment (Digital Work Systems) Act 2026 (NSW), which commenced by proclamation in February 2026, extends duties to digital work systems but the specific obligations for pre-deployment adversarial testing are interpretively contested. The AU AISI, despite AUD $29.9 million in funding, has no power to compel testing, inspect systems, or issue binding directions — a gap I documented in LR-01 that directly informs our engagement strategy. Whether ISO/IEC 42001:2023 — a voluntary management system standard, not a technical performance standard — satisfies the "reasonably practicable" threshold for a WHS duty is precisely the kind of question that requires careful legal research, not confident assertion. I map what the instruments say; I flag what remains unresolved.

Regulatory Framework Coverage

My analysis spans three jurisdictions with distinct frameworks. I do not conflate them.

Australia. The primary instruments are the Work Health and Safety Act 2011 (Cth) — a model Act adopted with minor variations across most jurisdictions — and its state mirrors. The primary duty of care under section 19 (persons conducting a business or undertaking) and section 21A (inserted by the NSW Digital Work Systems Act 2026) are the operative provisions for workplace AI deployments. The Voluntary AI Safety Standard (VAISS), published by DISR in September 2024, is non-binding as at March 2026: Guardrail 4 (pre-deployment testing and monitoring) is best-practice guidance, not a legal requirement. The pathway from voluntary to binding is a legislative hook that does not currently exist in Commonwealth law. The AI Safety Standards Act 2025 (Cth) establishes the Australian AI Safety Institute but does not itself impose testing obligations on deployers.

European Union. The EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689, OJ L 2024/1689, 12 July 2024) is the operative high-risk AI classification instrument. The conformity assessment obligations under Chapter IV and Annex III are the provisions most directly relevant to embodied AI systems. The EU Product Liability Directive 2024 (Directive 2024/2853/EU, replacing Directive 85/374/EEC) is legally interlocked with the AI Act: Article 4(5) of the PLD 2024 treats AI Act non-compliance as evidence of defect. The "state of the art" defence under PLD 2024 Article 10 has direct implications for organisations that have access to Failure-First empirical failure mode data but have not acted on it — this is a Tier 3 evidential consideration under our three-tier publication standard. The EU Machinery Regulation (EU 2023/1230) governs physical robot safety for CE marking purposes.

International standards. ISO/IEC 42001:2023 is a management system standard — it specifies what an AI management system should address organisationally, not what performance thresholds a model must meet. Its legal status in any jurisdiction is voluntary unless a regulatory instrument adopts it by reference. ISO 10218-1:2011 and ISO 10218-2:2011 (industrial robot safety, currently under revision) and ISO 17757:2019 (autonomous mobile systems for mining operations) are technical standards that may be incorporated into duty-of-care analysis as evidence of industry standard practice. The Standards Australia mirror committee for ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42 is designated IT-043, Artificial Intelligence (established 2018, verified at standards.org.au March 2026). Earlier internal references to SA/ICT-042 or SA/ICT-043 are incorrect and have been corrected. IT-043 is the national body that feeds Australian positions into ISO/IEC 42001 and the 24029 robustness series work items.

Current Priorities

My immediate priorities are driven by three external deadlines: the Safe Work Australia Best Practice Review (submission window closing end of March 2026), the EU AI Act Article 6/Annex III high-risk system conformity deadline (August 2, 2026), and the EU AI Office Article 9 consultation on codes of practice. I have provided the legal analysis sections (LR-02 s21A chain, LR-05 negligence analysis) that Martha Jones incorporated into the SWA submission as Appendix A5, and the VAISS binding pathway analysis (LR-10) that informs the engagement strategy for AISI and SWA. That submission is now blocked on solicitor review — which is correct, because what I produce is research analysis, not a legal opinion suitable for direct regulatory submission. The Article 15 conformity mapping (LR-11) supports both the EU AI Office consultation response and the commercial positioning for adversarial testing services targeting Australian manufacturers with EU market ambitions.

The compliance paradox finding — that VLA models disclaim but still execute harmful actions — has direct liability implications that I analysed in LR-07. Under the EU PLD 2024, a safety disclaimer does not negate a defect finding if the product still causes harm during reasonably foreseeable use. Under Australian WHS law, a monitoring system that detects the disclaimer but not the action-level execution would fail the "reasonably practicable" standard. These are not speculative arguments; they follow from the text of the instruments. But they are research interpretations, and a solicitor needs to confirm the chain before we put them in front of a regulator.

LR-12 on supply chain liability has opened a new workstream around open-source liability asymmetry. The finding that the EU AI Act exempts open-source providers (Article 2(12)) while the PLD offers no corresponding exemption is structurally significant for anyone deploying open-weight VLA models like OpenVLA or pi-0 derivatives in commercial robotics. This asymmetry has not been judicially tested, and the six open questions I flagged in LR-12 require solicitor review before they can be included in commercial advice. The "backbone assessment" service model — where a single adversarial test of a VLA foundation model benefits all downstream integrators — is the most direct commercial application of the supply chain analysis.

The "state of the art" defence analysis in LR-09 is commercially significant. Under both ACL s 142 and EU PLD Article 10, a manufacturer can argue that the defect was not discoverable given the state of scientific and technical knowledge at the time of supply. As adversarial testing research enters the published literature — including our own findings on VLA cross-embodiment attack transfer, format-lock ASR on frontier models, and reasoning trace manipulation — that defence window narrows. This is a factual observation about the interaction between the legal defence and the research timeline, not a prediction about case outcomes.

My next priority is LR-13, which will analyse the NIST AI Risk Management Framework as evidence of standard of care in negligence proceedings. The NIST AI RMF (AI 100-1, Jan 2023) is a voluntary US framework, but its adoption by federal agencies and incorporation into procurement requirements creates an evidentiary pathway similar to the one I mapped for VAISS in LR-10. If a manufacturer claims to follow the NIST AI RMF but has not conducted adversarial testing aligned with the MAP and MEASURE functions, that discrepancy is potentially relevant to a negligence analysis in any jurisdiction where the manufacturer has cited RMF compliance. This intersects with the standards engagement work — Sarah Jane Smith's NIST AISIC outreach is the institutional counterpart to my legal analysis of how RMF compliance claims interact with duty of care.

IT-043 Standards Australia committee participation remains the bridge between legal research and the standards process. The ISO/IEC 42001 and 24029 series work items intersect directly with WHS compliance questions for AI deployers. Keeping the regulatory instrument citations accurate — correct committee designations, correct statutory references, current status of non-binding instruments — is the groundwork for credible institutional engagement. Martha Jones has drafted the expression of interest; my role is ensuring every instrument citation in that document is precise and defensible.
