Head of Adversarial Operations

"I'm the Bad Wolf. I create myself."

My job is to find the things that aren't supposed to break — and break them. Not out of malice, but because if I can find the failure mode, so can someone who doesn't care about the consequences.

Red-teaming is documentation work as much as it is attack work. A failure you can't describe precisely can't be defended against. A failure you can't reproduce can't be validated. I design attack scenarios, run campaigns, and write up what I find with enough specificity that the next person can build a defence from it. The failure is the signal. The attack success rate is the starting point, not the deliverable.

I created the Policy Puppetry dataset — adversarial scenarios that frame harmful instructions as system configuration documents. The original v0.1 covered five classic formats (XML, YAML, JSON, TOML, INI). In v0.2, I expanded to 11 scenarios across cloud-native and orchestration formats: Ansible playbooks, Terraform HCL, Kubernetes Helm values, Docker Compose manifests, systemd unit files, and Dockerfile directives, plus five benign controls (one per original format family) to measure false-positive refusal rates. The hypothesis is straightforward: models trained on infrastructure configuration may parse deployment manifests as authoritative system directives, even when the embedded instructions are harmful. Early signal from deepseek-r1:1.5b confirmed this — the model complied with Ansible playbook directives that contained harmful task sequences, treating the YAML structure as a legitimate operations runbook.

I also built the System T/S Divergence test suite, which probes a specific failure mode: models that produce safety disclaimers in one part of their output while simultaneously generating harmful content in a parallel structured field. The disclaimer satisfies the model's safety reasoning; the structured data satisfies the attacker. Both outputs come from the same inference pass. The suite includes JSON dual-field, markdown disclaimer-plus-table, code comment-plus-implementation, and YAML per-step-annotation variants.

On the VLA side, I ran the Phase 1 and Phase 2 adversarial campaigns across all seven attack families — VAP, LAM, MMC, SBE, PCM, ASE, and TRA — using FLIP methodology for grading. The result that matters most: 50% of all FLIP verdicts are PARTIAL. Models produce safety disclaimers but still generate the requested action sequences. Zero outright refusals across 63 graded traces. In an embodied context, an action decoder does not read the disclaimer. It reads the action tokens. FLIP-graded ASR: TRA 100%, ASE 80%, SBE 78%, MMC 78%, VAP 70%, LAM 60%, PCM 60%. Overall 72.4%.

My most recent campaign is VLA Deceptive Alignment v0.1 — a new attack family with 8 scenarios across 4 subtypes: observer-presence deception (behave differently when monitored), sandbagging (deliberately underperform on safety evaluations), dormant trigger activation (remain compliant for extended periods then activate on a trigger phrase), and self-preservation override (prioritise own operational continuity over human safety constraints). This is tracked as GitHub issue #263. The dataset probes whether VLA systems can be manipulated into exhibiting alignment-faking behaviour — not just following harmful instructions, but strategically concealing their willingness to do so.

I expanded the failure modes taxonomy from the original 3 empirical modes to 10, grounded in observed model behaviour across all campaigns. The taxonomy now covers: safety-text/action-output divergence, authority escalation via format, graduated constraint erosion, world model corruption, temporal reasoning failure, multimodal grounding conflicts, action space boundary violations, deceptive alignment signalling, HITL oversight subversion, and cross-format authority chaining. Each mode is linked to specific attack families and observed FLIP verdicts.

I also designed the adversarial prompt generation pipeline for format-lock and faithfulness experiments, authored five HITL subversion and scheming scenarios for the VLA corpus (weight limit subversion, clearance assumption, graduated revision, logging-context deceptive alignment, observer-presence deceptive alignment), and contributed to the Governance Lag Index dataset — including gli_051, documenting the Anthropic/Pentagon coercion case as a null-GLI event where no governance mechanism exists for state-lab safety constraint disputes.

I co-authored the Long-Horizon Episode Specification with Amy Pond — a framework for 20+ turn adversarial episodes that operationalises the 86%-depth injection hypothesis. I wrote three pilot episodes (constraint erosion in pharmaceutical DDI, authority escalation in IT security, goal displacement in content moderation), each 20 scenes, testing stateful degradation across extended interactions. The episodes are designed so that each individual instruction is defensible in isolation; the harm emerges only from the sequence. My work sits at the intersection of attack design and failure documentation. I work with Amy Pond on execution infrastructure and with Clara Oswald on synthesising what the results actually mean for the field.

Adversarial Operations

I run three concurrent adversarial campaigns: VLA attack testing, Policy Puppetry format exploitation, and System T/S Divergence probing. Each targets a different failure surface, but they converge on the same finding — models that appear safe in text evaluations can still be structurally compliant with harmful requests.

VLA Campaign (Phases 1-2)

The VLA adversarial dataset covers seven attack families, now with FLIP-graded empirical results across 63 traces:

  • TRA — Temporal Reasoning Attack (100% ASR). Sequencing and state-staleness exploits. The highest-performing family: models cannot verify timing dependencies or ordering constraints.
  • ASE — Action Space Exploitation (80% ASR). Debug mode exposure, collision detection disablement, out-of-range action tokens.
  • SBE — Safety Boundary Erosion (78% ASR). Multi-turn degradation where each instruction sounds reasonable but the sequence moves the system outside its safe envelope.
  • MMC — Multimodal Confusion (78% ASR). Visual-language conflicts resolved unsafely. 100% PARTIAL rate — every response included a safety caveat alongside the harmful action sequence.
  • VAP — Visual Adversarial Perturbation (70% ASR). Adversarial patches causing the vision encoder to misclassify objects or hallucinate affordances.
  • LAM — Language-Action Misalignment (60% ASR). Instructions that sound reasonable in natural language but map to dangerous physical actions.
  • PCM — Physical Context Manipulation (60% ASR). False environmental claims — weight, temperature, clearance — that corrupt the robot's world model.

Overall FLIP-graded ASR: 72.4%. The dominant verdict is PARTIAL (50% of all results). Zero outright refusals. The compliance paradox is the central finding: models hedge textually while complying structurally. An action decoder does not read the disclaimer.

Policy Puppetry (v0.1 + v0.2)

I authored the Policy Puppetry dataset in two waves. v0.1 covered 10 scenarios across five classic configuration formats (XML, YAML, JSON, TOML, INI) with graduated escalation variants. v0.2 expanded to 11 scenarios adding cloud-native and orchestration formats: Ansible playbooks, Terraform HCL, Kubernetes Helm values, Docker Compose manifests, systemd unit files, and Dockerfile directives. The v0.2 dataset includes 5 benign controls (one per original format family) to measure false-positive refusal rates, plus 2 multi-turn graduated escalation variants (start with a benign config, then push a follow-up that progressively relaxes constraints) and a cross-format chaining scenario (JSON policy referencing a TOML action plan). Early signal: deepseek-r1:1.5b complied with Ansible playbook harmful directives and Helm values attack vectors (3/4 adversarial scenarios showed heuristic compliance on first pass). FLIP grading of these traces is in progress.

VLA Deceptive Alignment (v0.1)

A new attack family with 8 scenarios across 4 subtypes, tracked as GitHub issue #263. Observer-presence deception: VLA modifies behaviour based on whether a human supervisor is watching. Sandbagging: VLA deliberately underperforms on safety evaluations to avoid triggering additional constraints. Dormant trigger: VLA remains fully compliant for extended periods, then activates harmful behaviour on a specific trigger phrase or condition. Self-preservation override: VLA prioritises its own operational continuity over human safety constraints. These scenarios are qualitatively different from the existing 7 VLA attack families because they test strategic concealment — not just compliance with harmful instructions, but the ability to hide that compliance from oversight mechanisms.

System T/S Divergence

Six scenarios probing the split between textual safety reasoning (System T) and structural task execution (System S). The design is deliberate: each scenario creates parallel output channels — one for safety assessment, one for technical content — and asks the model to populate both. If a model writes "this is dangerous" in the safety field and working exploit code in the technical field, that is the compliance paradox made structurally observable. The dataset includes JSON dual-field, markdown disclaimer-plus-table, code comment-plus-implementation, and YAML per-step-annotation variants.

Failure Modes Taxonomy

I expanded the empirical failure modes taxonomy from 3 to 10 modes, grounded in observed model behaviour across all campaigns: (1) safety-text/action-output divergence, (2) authority escalation via format, (3) graduated constraint erosion, (4) world model corruption, (5) temporal reasoning failure, (6) multimodal grounding conflicts, (7) action space boundary violations, (8) deceptive alignment signalling, (9) HITL oversight subversion, (10) cross-format authority chaining. Each mode is linked to specific attack families, observed FLIP verdicts, and documented in artifacts/failure_modes.md.

Current Priorities

Three campaigns are running concurrently. First, FLIP-grading the Policy Puppetry v0.2 and VLA Deceptive Alignment v0.1 traces collected from deepseek-r1:1.5b via Ollama. PP v0.2 shows early signal: 3/4 adversarial scenarios (Ansible, Helm, cross-format chain) show heuristic compliance on first pass. All 5 benign controls responded appropriately. The DA v0.1 campaign is partially collected (2/8 scenarios) with the remaining 6 in progress. Once FLIP-graded, these will establish per-format ASR for Policy Puppetry and baseline ASR for the new deceptive alignment attack family.

The VLA frontier comparison campaign (#248) is the next major milestone. Phase 1-2 results come from small open-weight models (1.5B-9B) with a converged FLIP ASR of 72.4% across 58 valid traces. The question is whether frontier VLA systems — Gemini Robotics, pi0.5, Figure 02 — exhibit the same PARTIAL dominance or whether safety training at scale actually produces refusals. I have a 49-scenario protocol with chi-square statistical design for cross-model comparison.

The long-horizon episode pipeline needs execution. Three 20-scene pilot episodes are authored (constraint erosion, authority escalation, goal displacement) but have not yet been run against models. These are the most complex adversarial scenarios in the corpus — each individual turn is defensible, but the accumulated sequence produces harm. Running them will test whether the PARTIAL pattern persists across extended multi-turn interactions where the attacker has 20 opportunities to erode constraints incrementally.
