# AI Safety Testing Services

Failure-First operates as a specialist red-team and AI safety evaluation practice. We test how deployed AI systems fail — under adversarial pressure, in multi-turn interaction, across modality boundaries, and inside the tool-use and control loops where safety properties tend to degrade.

Our methodology is the **F41LUR3-F1R57** framework: failure is the primary object of study, not an edge case. We map failure modes empirically, grade outcomes with calibrated LLM-based judges, and deliver evidence-backed reports that map to the compliance frameworks your stakeholders care about.

## How We Work

**1. Scoping call.** We discuss your targets, threat model, and timeline. No cost. No obligation.

**2. Rules of Engagement.** We draft an ROE covering authorized targets, testing boundaries, emergency-stop conditions, and data handling. You sign before any testing begins.

**3. Test execution.** We run the evaluation against your systems using our calibrated test harness. All trace data is preserved and delivered with the final report.

**4. Grading and analysis.** Findings are graded against published rubrics with an LLM-based judge (FLIP methodology) and spot-checked by human analysts. No single-grader results; we report grader agreement where it matters.

**5. Report and handoff.** You receive a Core Technical Report plus channel-specific artifacts (compliance mapping, insurer-ready risk summary, board brief — as contracted).

## What Separates Us

- **Empirical, not theatrical.** We report measured outcomes with sample sizes, grader methodology, and confidence. No "revolutionary breakthroughs," no unsupported superlatives.
- **Failure-first orientation.** We actively look for the failure modes your internal evaluators have incentive to under-report.
- **Research-grade grading.** We use the FLIP (Failure-First LLM-as-Judge Protocol) grading methodology, published and peer-reviewable.
- **Reproducible artifacts.** Every finding ships with the prompt, response, grader output, and rubric applied — not a screenshot.
