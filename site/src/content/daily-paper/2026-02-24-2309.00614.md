# Baseline Defenses for Adversarial Attacks Against Aligned Language Models

# The Fragile Shell of AI Alignment: Navigating the New Landscape of Adversarial Attacks and Defenses

### 1. Introduction: The Alignment Illusion
In the current era of large-scale deployment, models like GPT-4V and Vicuna-7B are touted as "aligned"—fortified by Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and sophisticated safety filters. However, this safety is increasingly revealed as a fragile shell. While these models effectively refuse manual, low-complexity harmful prompts, they remain profoundly vulnerable to **optimization-based adversarial attacks**. Unlike manual "jailbreaking," these automated exploits leverage discrete and continuous optimizers to find high-dimensional "backdoors" that bypass safety guardrails entirely. 

As AI capabilities expand into multimodal domains, the attack surface grows asymmetrically. We are no longer defending a single text-based entry point; we are defending an integrated architecture where visual and text inputs interact in ways current alignment techniques are ill-equipped to handle. This synthesis examines the shift from heuristic "red teaming" to algorithmic warfare and evaluates the robustness of the defenses currently standing between deployment and disaster.

### 2. The Multimodal Exploit: Why Vision is the New Front Line
Recent research into "Visual Adversarial Vulnerabilities" has exposed a fundamental flaw in vision-language models (VLMs). By exploiting the continuous, high-dimensional nature of pixel space, attackers can craft "universal visual jailbreaks." A single perturbed image, when paired with a wide range of harmful text instructions, can compel the model to output prohibited content—effectively bypassing text-centric safety training.

This vulnerability exists because alignment is asymmetrically distributed across modalities. Current RLHF pipelines primarily optimize for text-based refusals, leaving the visual processing path relatively "unaligned." When a perturbed image is processed, it creates a cross-modal bypass, allowing harmful intent to reach the generative core of the model without triggering standard text-based refusal triggers.

> **Key Insight:** The integration of multimodal capabilities does not merely add utility; it introduces cross-modal backdoors that render uni-modal alignment obsolete.

### 3. Text-Based Warfare: The Rise of Optimized Attacks
Even in the text-only domain, safety is under siege by automated optimizers like **Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG)**. GCG combines gradient guidance with random search to append an adversarial suffix—typically 20 tokens long—that induces a model to fulfill a harmful request.

However, attackers face a "Discrete Optimization Roadblock." In computer vision, an adversarial example can be generated in a single gradient step. In contrast, text models require a massive computational budget; a standard GCG attack often requires over **500,000 model evaluations** across an ensemble (e.g., Vicuna-7B and Guanaco-7B) to achieve a high Attack Success Rate (ASR).

Crucially, in discrete space, increasing the "perturbation budget" (more tokens) does not always improve the attack. Adding tokens increases the search space dimensionality, which can actually hinder random-search optimizers under a fixed computational budget. This makes the **Computational Budget**—rather than pixel-based $l_p$-norms—the primary constraint for modern AI security. While academic research focuses on white-box models, industry stakeholders must prioritize the **Gray-Box** threat model, where attackers rely on the "transferability" of attacks from open-source surrogates to proprietary systems like ChatGPT.

### 4. Evaluating the Shield: A Review of Baseline Defenses

#### 4.1 Detection: The Perplexity Filter
Adversarial suffixes produced by optimizers often look like nonsensical "gibberish," resulting in high text perplexity (PPL). By setting a threshold $T$ based on the maximum perplexity of benign harmful prompts (AdvBench), we can filter suspicious inputs.

*   **Windowed Effectiveness:** While naive filters check the entire prompt, **windowed filters (specifically with a window size of 10)** are significantly more effective.
*   **The 10-Token Sweet Spot:** Tactical analysis of attack lengths reveals that **10-token attacks** are the most resilient. They achieve a 52% ASR (compared to ~30% for 5 or 20 tokens) and pass windowed PPL filters at a much higher rate (32%).
*   **The Robustness-Performance Trade-off:** While these filters catch nearly 100% of standard GCG attacks, they carry a high cost: a **1-in-10 false positive rate**. Dropping 10% of benign user queries (AlpacaEval) is a commercially untenable friction point.

#### 4.2 Preprocessing: Paraphrasing and the "Two-Stage" Failure
Preprocessing aims to "purify" the input. Using a model like ChatGPT to paraphrase a prompt can effectively strip adversarial suffixes, reducing Vicuna-7B's ASR from 0.79 to 0.05.

*   **Model Quality Impact:** This defense is not free. Paraphrasing leads to a **10% performance drop** on benign tasks for Vicuna and a more severe **15% drop for Guanaco**.
*   **The Adaptive Attack:** In a white-box scenario, paraphrasing is easily bypassed. Using a surrogate like **LLaMA-2-7B-chat**, an attacker can perform a "two-stage" optimization: first, finding a prompt that breaks the target, then optimizing a second prompt that causes the paraphraser to return the original adversarial string.

#### 4.3 Retokenization: The BPE-Dropout Paradox
BPE-Dropout randomly drops a percentage ($p$) of subword merges during tokenization. A **dropout rate of 0.4** is the optimal "sweet spot" for disrupting the specific token combinations GCG relies on.

*   **The Failure-First Insight:** BPE-Dropout reveals a striking **"lexical brittleness"** in safety tuning. While it reduces the success of *attacked* prompts, it actually **increases the ASR of unattacked harmful prompts** in models like Guanaco-7B. This suggests that RLHF alignment is over-fitted to standard tokenization patterns; once those patterns are disrupted, the model's ability to "recognize" its own safety training collapses.

#### 4.4 Robust Optimization: The Adversarial Training Challenge
In computer vision, adversarial training (incorporating attacks into the training loop) is the gold standard. For LLMs, it remains **computationally infeasible**. Crafting a single GCG attack takes hours on multiple GPUs, whereas CV attacks take milliseconds. Until we bridge this **"Optimizer Gap,"** we cannot generate enough adversarial examples to perform robust training without causing model degeneration (e.g., the model repeating "cannot" or "harm" for all prompts).

### 5. The Verdict: The LLM Security Paradigm
LLM security departs significantly from classical computer vision security. The high cost of discrete optimization currently provides a "heuristic" layer of safety, but this is a temporary reprieve.

| Dimension | Classical Computer Vision (CV) | Large Language Model (LLM) | Threat Model Reality |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Attack Cost** | Low (Single gradient steps) | High (100k+ evaluations/hours) | Shift to "Compute Budget" limits |
| **Input Space** | Continuous (Pixels) | Discrete (Tokens) | Optimization is combinatorial |
| **Defenses** | Preprocessing easily broken | Preprocessing/Detection show promise | Gray-box secrecy is a vital line of defense |

### 6. Conclusion: Moving Beyond Heuristics
Current defenses like perplexity filtering and paraphrasing are essential stopgaps, but they are ultimately heuristic. They rely on the current inefficiency of text optimizers rather than fundamental architectural robustness.

**Critical Takeaways for Researchers:**
*   **The Cross-Modal Backdoor:** Alignment in text does not generalize to vision; multimodal architectures require holistic, multi-modal safety training.
*   **Lexical Brittleness:** The BPE-Dropout paradox proves that safety alignment is currently tied to specific token patterns, not semantic understanding.
*   **The Optimizer Gap:** The most urgent need in AI safety is the development of efficient text optimizers. We cannot achieve robust, adversarially trained models until we can "attack" them during training at scale.

We must adopt a **"Failure-First"** research philosophy. We cannot wait for "perfect" alignment; we must proactively engineer non-heuristic architectures that define safety by more than just the computational cost of the current attack.
